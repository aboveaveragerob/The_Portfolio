### Prepare the Damaged Wall

Remove all loose material

- For stubborn areas, mix 1 part vinegar to 2 parts boiling water and spray, then let sit for 15 mins and scrape off with a putty knife
- Feather any sharp ridges with 80–120 grit so the surface is flat to the touch.

### Seal the torn drywall

*Locks down the fuzzy paper and old adhesive so your joint compound doesn’t blister and so the wall doesn’t flash through your topcoat*

- 1 quart Zinsser Gardz Problem Surface Sealer ($15-20 at Home Depot, in the primer aisle—it’s a clear/milky liquid in a can) and 3/8” nap roller (Kilz Original oil-based primer does the same thing but smells MUCH worse)
- Paint the wall with it like normal with the sealer, using a brush for corners. It will go on milky white, but dry clear in 30 mins

### Skim coat the damaged wall

*When you run your hand (eyes closed) across the wall, you shouldn’t feel edges or dips.*

- Materials
    - 1 bucket USG All-Purpose Joint Compound with dark green lid (4.5 gallon bucket, $10-12)
        - Home Depot note: There will be MANY options. You want ready-mix (already wet in a bucket, not powder), and specifically the “All-Purpose” with the dark green lid. NOT  “lightweight” or “topping”
    - Second bucket for second coat: USG Plus 3 Lightweight (lighter to sand)
    - Paint roller with 3/4” nap cover (this is thicker than normal—look for “high nap” or “heavy nap”)
    - 12” or 14” drywall taping knife (wide as you can find)
    - Small bucket of water
    - (optional) Power drill + paddle mixer attachment
- Steps
    - How to mix the compound
        1. Open your fresh bucket of joint compound (NEVER use an old opened bucket—dried chunks will ruin everything)
        2. It will look like thick peanut butter
        3. Add about 1 cup of water
        4. Stir with the paddle mixer (or a strong spoon) until it looks like thick yogurt or pancake batter
        5. You want it to pour slowly off your taping knife, not plop off in chunks
    - How to apply it (First Coat)
        1. Pour mixed compound into your roller tray
        2. Roll it onto the wall in sections (work about 3 feet at a time)
        3. Roll it on THICK—you shouldn’t see the wall color through it
        4. Immediately take your 12” taping knife and smooth it:
        5. Hold the knife at about a 30° angle
        6. Drag it across the wet compound in long, smooth strokes
        7. Overlap your strokes
            1. Don’t worry about perfection - just get it relatively flat
        8. Feather any ridges
            1. After spreading the mud, position the taping knife so 2/3 of it is over the mud and 1/3 is over bare wall
            2. Press DOWN HARD on the bare wall side of the knife
            3. Keep ZERO pressure on the mud side
            4. Drag across to squish the edge paper-thin and spread it out
            
            *If it dries and a ridge forms, apply a layer of thin mud on top of the ridge and feather it again (new mud will reactivate the old mud)*
            
        9. Let it dry completely (24 hours in winter, 12 hours in summer)
    - Prepare for second coat
        1. Once dry, take a 6” putty knife and gently scrape off any high ridges or bumps
            1. You should NOT need to sand between coats—just knock down the obvious bumps
        2. Wipe dust off with a damp cloth
    - How to apply second coat
        1. Mix your Plus 3 Lightweight compound a bit THINNER this time (more like thin yogurt)
        2. Repeat the rolling and smoothing process
        3. This coat should be thinner
        4. Let dry 24 hours
    - Checking your work
        - Run your hand across the wall with your eyes closed to look for ridges or dips
        - Shine a bright flashlight at an angle across the wall; shadows will show imperfections
    - Sand the skim coat
        1. Get and cut sanding screen 150 grit to fit a pole sander pad
        2. Hold the pole with both hands and press the pad flat against the wall
        3. Use light pressure and circular motions; let the screen do its job, don’t press 
        4. Vacuum dust as you go

## Prime the Walls

1-2 gallons Zinsser Bulls Eye 1-2-3 Primer or Kilz PVA Primer (white, water-based)

1. Make sure to get “drywall primer” or “PVA primer”
2. Use 3/8” nap roller and apply ONE coat of primer - it’s fine if it looks splotchy or thin
</aside>

<aside>

## Caulk the Trim

- Use acrylic latex white caulk - NOT silicone or anything clear, they’re not paintable - and caulk all trim, gaps, and holes
    - Look for “Alex Plus” or “DAP Alex”
    - Wet your finger and smooth the bead within 30 seconds of application
    - Let dry for a full 24 hours
</aside>

<aside>

## Paint the Walls

*Tip: Paint the borders and trim first by cutting inwards 3” from the frame. Then work in 3’x3’ squares that DO NOT overlap the cuts. At the end, while the paint is still drying, then overlap the cut with the interior paint to blend them*

- 2 coats of paint with 3/8-1/2” nap rollers should be more than enough coverage
